Silver Oak · Silky Oak
Grevillea robusta A.Cunn. ex R.Br. · Family: Proteaceae
Medicinal Timber Ornamental
🌿 Traditional Medicine
Documented Kenyan uses (Kakamega Forest) include treatment of sore throat, earache, chest complaints, flu, and toothache. Bark and leaf extracts show anti-inflammatory and hepatoprotective properties in pharmacological studies. Flowers are a rich nectar source.
⚠️ Correction: The original galactagogue/breastmilk claim for this species has no verified ethnobotanical source in Kenyan literature and has been removed.
🪵 Timber & Craft
Valued for furniture, cabinetry, plywood, and turnery. Attractive, durable wood used for decorative paneling and musical instruments.
🌱 Excellent coffee-shade tree and erosion-control species in highland agroforestry systems. Note: not nitrogen-fixing. Native to SE Queensland–NE New South Wales, Australia.
Doum Palm · Thika Palm
Hyphaene compressa H.Wendl. · Family: Arecaceae
Food Medicinal Fibre
🍎 Edible & Nutrition
Fibrous, sweet fruit pulp eaten fresh or made into a drink. The unripe endosperm (vegetable ivory) is edible; the kernel provides a nutritious snack. A staple food in Kenya's arid and semi-arid regions.
💊 Traditional Medicine
Fruit pulp used as a remedy for intestinal worms and digestive complaints. Applied in anti-inflammatory preparations.
🧺 Handicrafts & Construction
Leaves woven into baskets, mats, brooms, and roofing thatch. The trunk provides building poles, beehives, and cabinet-making material. A true multipurpose palm of the ASALs.
🐘 Fruits consumed by elephants and baboons — critical for seed dispersal and ecosystem services.
African Cherry · Red Stinkwood
Prunus africana (Hook.f.) Kalkman · Family: Rosaceae
Medicinal Timber Conservation
💊 Critical Medicinal Tree
Bark extract (marketed as "pygeum") is globally recognised for treating benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH). Used for generations in African traditional medicine; also shows anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and hepatoprotective activities in pharmacological research.
🪵 Durable Timber
Hard, heavy heartwood with a distinctive reddish-brown colour; used for construction, flooring, and tool handles. The bruised bark, leaves, and fruit have a characteristic smell.
⚠️ IUCN Red List: Vulnerable (VU). Bark over-harvesting for the European herbal market is the primary threat. Nursery propagation is essential — Oloolua CFA plays a direct conservation role.
Avocado
Persea americana Mill. · Family: Lauraceae
Superfood Medicinal Cosmetic
🥑 Nutritional Powerhouse
Rich in monounsaturated fats (primarily oleic acid), potassium, magnesium, and bioactive polyphenols supporting cardiovascular health. Pulp, peel, and seeds provide antioxidants and anti-inflammatory compounds.
🧴 Medicinal & Cosmetic Uses
Leaf and seed extracts used in traditional medicine for skin infections, digestive disorders, and blood-sugar regulation. Avocado oil is valued in skincare and hair products for its high penetration and regenerative lipid profile.
💚 Key agroforestry species — widely intercropped with coffee in Kenyan highlands, providing canopy shade and a high-value cash fruit.
Matomoko · Common Wild Fig
Ficus thonningii Blume · Family: Moraceae
Fodder Medicinal Shade
🐐 Fodder & Livestock Feed
Leaves and young figs are excellent dry-season fodder. Research shows inclusion of F. thonningii leaves can replace a significant portion of commercial concentrate in goat diets while supporting weight gain.
💊 Bark & Latex Medicine
Bark decoction used for colds, sore throat, dysentery, and constipation. Latex applied topically for wound healing. Also used traditionally as a galactagogue (to stimulate milk production).
🌿 Shade & Soil Stabilisation
Ideal live fence, soil stabiliser, and shade tree. Fig fruits support birds and frugivorous wildlife, enhancing forest biodiversity.
Markhamia · Nile Tulip Tree
Markhamia lutea (Benth.) K.Schum. · Family: Bignoniaceae
Timber Medicinal Bee Forage
🪵 Versatile Timber
Light but durable wood used for light construction, furniture, tool handles, and beehives. Fast-growing; well-suited to agroforestry boundary planting.
💊 Traditional Remedies
Root bark used for anaemia, diarrhoea, and back pain. Leaf preparations applied for oedema and rheumatic complaints. Extracts show documented antimicrobial, anticancer, and anti-inflammatory activity in pharmacological studies.
🐝 Ornamental & Bee Forage
Vivid yellow trumpet-shaped flowers are highly attractive to bees — an important species for honey production alongside ornamental and soil-conservation planting.
Acacia (Vachellia)
Vachellia spp. (formerly Acacia) · Family: Fabaceae
Gum Fodder Tannin
🌱 Edible Gum & Fodder
Pods and leaves are highly palatable livestock fodder. Gum arabic (from V. senegal) has food-grade and pharmaceutical applications. Bark fibres used for rope and tannin extraction.
💊 Medicinal Bark
Bark decoction used for diarrhoea, gingivitis, and skin irritation. Applied as an astringent and for wound healing. Chewing twigs strengthens gum tissue.
🪵 Dense Timber & Fuelwood
Extremely hard, dense wood prized for furniture, carving, fencing posts, and high-quality firewood and charcoal. Nitrogen-fixing root nodules improve soil fertility.
🌍 Note: the genus Acacia was split in 2005 — African species are now correctly classified as Vachellia or Senegalia.
Teclea · Small Fruited Teclea
Teclea nobilis Del. · Family: Rutaceae
Analgesic Antipyretic Anti-inflammatory
💊 Potent Medicinal Tree
Leaves, bark, and roots used across East Africa to treat malaria, rheumatism, arthritis, pneumonia, cough, fever, and headache. The triterpenoid compound lupeol is responsible for strong analgesic, antipyretic, and anti-inflammatory effects documented in pharmacological studies.
🌡️ Traditional Applications
Leaf decoction taken for chest pain and as an anthelmintic. Root infusions used for stomach-ache, backache, and respiratory complaints. Steam baths using heated leaf preparations used to treat fevers.
🧪 Pharmacological studies confirm CNS depressant activity and low acute toxicity at therapeutic doses.
Chestnut
Castanea sativa Mill. (European/introduced) · Family: Fagaceae
Edible Nut Timber Astringent
🍽️ Nutritious Staple
Nuts are rich in complex carbohydrates and low in fat compared to other tree nuts. Eaten roasted, boiled, or ground into gluten-free flour. Historically resilient as a famine-period crop in European and Asian highlands.
💊 Traditional Medicine
Leaves have mild sedative properties; bark and leaf tannins used as an astringent for diarrhoea and whooping cough. Chestnut wood and leaf extracts show antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory activity in research.
🪵 Durable Timber
Naturally rot-resistant due to high tannin content; used for fencing posts, furniture, and outdoor construction. Also used as a substrate for shiitake mushroom cultivation.
📌 Note: Castanea species are introduced to Kenya. If this nursery holds an African equivalent, it may be Calophyllum inophyllum (Alexandrian Laurel) or another highland species — please verify against nursery records.
Drypetes
Drypetes spp. · Family: Putranjivaceae
Ethnomedicine Pest Control Fish Poison
💊 Ethnomedicine & Ritual Use
Various Drypetes species are used across Africa to treat fevers, insect bites, and as a general restorative. Some species serve as stimulants. Root and stem bark are used to protect stored grain from pest insects.
🌱 Reproductive & Antifungal
Employed traditionally to support fertility and treat reproductive complaints. Stem bark extracts of East African species show antifungal activity including against Candida strains in in-vitro studies.
⚠️ Several Drypetes species contain potent bioactive compounds — traditional use requires expert guidance. Verify the specific local species in nursery records for accurate use documentation.
Croton · Musine
Croton megalocarpus Hutch. · Family: Euphorbiaceae
Biofuel Medicinal Mulch
🛢️ Biodiesel & Animal Feed
Seeds contain 30–40% oil (EATEC Kenya data: 30–32% for non-edible seeds), suitable for biodiesel via transesterification. The residual seed cake contains 25–30% protein and is used as a poultry and livestock feed supplement — comparable to canola cake.
📌 Correction: The original "50% protein" figure refers to one outlier dataset. Confirmed protein content in seed cake is 25–30% (Wikipedia / CIFOR / Croton seed cake studies).
💊 Traditional Healing
Bark decoction used against intestinal worms, whooping cough, pneumonia, malaria, stomach ailments, and spleen and gall bladder complaints. Leaves used for wound healing and fever management.
🌿 Mulch & Soil Health
High-nitrogen leaves make excellent mulch for crops, especially coffee. Fast-growing pioneer species ideal for forest restoration and carbon sequestration (estimated 400 kg CO₂ over its lifespan).
Toothbrush Tree · Miswak
Salvadora persica L. · Family: Salvadoraceae
Oral Hygiene Antibacterial Traditional
🪥 Natural Toothbrush
Twigs (miswak/siwak) used for centuries as a natural toothbrush with documented antibacterial, antiplaque, and anti-gingivitis properties. Rich in fluoride, silica, salvadorine, and tannins — components that support oral health.
💊 Systemic Medicine
Root decoctions used for spleen conditions, stomach-ache, and kidney complaints. Leaves applied for cough, asthma, and rheumatism. Hypoglycaemic (blood-sugar lowering) effects confirmed in pharmacological studies.
🌿 Traditional dental use predates modern dentistry and is still widespread across East Africa, the Horn, and the Middle East.
East African Greenheart · Muthiga
Warburgia ugandensis Sprague · Family: Canellaceae
Anti-malarial Antifungal Timber
💊 Highly Valued Medicinal Tree
Bark and root extracts used for pneumonia, asthma, malaria, candidiasis, diarrhoea, and skin infections. The peppery-bitter taste comes from an abundance of drimane-type sesquiterpenes including warburganal, mukaadial, and ugandensolide — responsible for strong antimicrobial, antifungal, and antiplasmodial activity.
🍂 Traditional Applications
Bark chewed for stomach-ache and constipation. Leaves historically used as a curry spice in Kenya (early Indian railway workers used them before chilli became common). Smoke from burning bark inhaled for chest complaints; powdered bark snuffed for sinus complaints and colds.
🪵 Timber & Resin
Yellow-green heartwood, very fragrant when freshly cut and highly resistant to insect attack. Resin used as glue for tool handles. Also planted as an ornamental shade tree.
📌 Note: The correct primary English trade name in Kenya is "East African Greenheart" (Kikuyu: Muthiga). "Pepper-bark tree" is a secondary common name shared with related Warburgia species.
⚠️ Bark over-harvesting threatens wild populations — careful harvesting and nursery propagation are essential for conservation.
Jackfruit
Artocarpus heterophyllus Lam. · Family: Moraceae
Giant Fruit Timber Antioxidant
🍈 World's Largest Tree Fruit
Nutritious pulp rich in carbohydrates, protein, vitamins A, B, and C, and minerals. Seeds are boiled or roasted as a snack. Unripe fruit used as a vegetable (widely used as a meat substitute); ripe fruit is sweet and aromatic.
💊 Medicinal Uses
Bark, root, and leaf preparations used for skin disorders and wound healing. Documented antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activity. Latex has wound-healing properties and has been used to treat snakebite in traditional medicine.
🪵 Durable Timber
Heartwood is naturally termite-resistant and valued for high-quality furniture, musical instruments (e.g. veena, tabla shells), and construction. Leaves and by-products provide cattle fodder.
Sisal
Agave sisalana Perrine · Family: Asparagaceae
Hard Fibre Ornamental Erosion Control
工業 Rope & Fibre Industry
One of the world's primary sources of hard natural fibre, used for rope, twine, carpets, and handicrafts. Leaf fibres resist saltwater corrosion, making sisal invaluable for marine and agricultural applications.
🎨 Ornamental & Erosion Control
Architectural succulent used for landscaping, live fencing, and slope stabilisation. Drought-tolerant and able to grow on degraded or marginal soils — strong fit for ASAL restoration.
💊 Minor Medicinal Use
Sap and leaf extracts used in traditional preparations for skin ailments and as a diuretic, though this is secondary to its fibre applications.
📌 Note: Sisal is an agave/succulent, not a tree. It is correctly listed as a nursery plant but the "tree" classification in this catalogue should be updated to "multipurpose plant".
♻️ Natural, biodegradable fibre — a sustainable alternative to synthetic nylon and polypropylene ropes. Kenya is a significant sisal exporter.
